Abstract. The fine structure constant \(\alpha = 1/137.036...\) is a transcendental number. In K6/QS3, the ratio \(a_0/t_C = 1/\alpha\) — Bohr radius to reduced Compton wavelength — is experimental evidence that \(\alpha\) is a topological ratio, not an algebraic one. This paper documents the systematic accounting of electromagnetic coupling corrections from K6/QS3 geometry, without using \(\alpha\) as an input. Three alpha-free corrections are computed (log running, Hopf Hodge, and K₃,₃ Hodge), accounting for 37% of the required adjustment. Three further terms — geodesic curvature on S³, 5-valent vertex junction corrections, and temporal Hodge weights — are identified and set up for future computation. The goal is not algebraic derivation of a transcendental number, but geometric consistency accounting.

What is K6/QS3?

The complete graph K6 — six vertices, every pair connected, 15 edges total — together with the quaternionic 3-sphere QS3 (isomorphic to SU(2)) form the discrete geometric substrate of this research program. Without assuming continuous spacetime and without inserting coupling constants by hand, the gauge symmetries of the Standard Model emerge from this structure: U(1) from the Hopf fibration of QS3, SU(2) from its quaternionic generators, and SU(3) from the K₃,₃ bipartite subgraph. The Lorentz group acts through SL(2,\(\mathbb{C}\)) holonomies, with causal structure emerging from the stationary phase of the path sum. All from one finite graph.

Two free parameters — tube length \(\lambda\) and stiffness \(k\) — are fixed by the electron mass alone. The speed of light emerges as an exact algebraic output. The framework is falsifiable: every claim is a specific numerical prediction to be checked. The papers in this series document the current state of that checking.

1. Alpha is Topological, Not Algebraic

The fine structure constant \(\alpha = 1/137.036...\) is transcendental. A transcendental number cannot emerge from integer counting, winding numbers, or Euler characteristics. It requires geometric integration. The K6/QS3 framework acknowledges this honestly: the goal is to account for the electromagnetic coupling through identifiable geometric contributions, not to derive an exact algebraic formula.

The starting observation is geometric:

\[ \frac{a_0}{t_C} = \frac{1}{\alpha} = 137.036 \]
The hydrogen atom is roughly 137 times larger than the electron's relativistic quantum length. This ratio is experimental evidence that \(\alpha\) is topological in origin.

In K6/QS3, \(t_C\) sets the relay node spacing \(a\). The ratio \(a_0/a\) counts how many relay nodes fit inside the hydrogen atom — a topological statement about how the Hopf fiber U(1) coupling scales across the hydrogen ground state. The irrationality of this ratio is expected: it involves transcendental geometric integrals (the Seifert surface, the dilogarithm) that cannot be rational.

2. The K6 Bare Estimate and Required Correction

From K6 loop geometry and the self-consistency condition (Part XXVI of working notes), the bare topological estimate gives \(1/\alpha_{K6} \approx 138.4\). The known value is \(1/\alpha = 137.036\). The required correction is:

\[ \Delta(1/\alpha)_\text{required} = -(138.4 - 137.036) = -1.364 \]

Caveat: the 138.4 estimate itself used the known \(\alpha\) in the self-consistency equation and is therefore not fully alpha-free. A genuine forward prediction requires computing the bare coupling from K6 topology with no \(\alpha\) on the right-hand side. This is an open problem. The corrections documented here are, however, independently motivated by the K6/QS3 geometry.

5. Three Open Terms

5.1 Geodesic Curvature Correction

The edges of K6 on S³ are geodesic arcs. A geodesic on S³ has zero intrinsic (geodesic) curvature, but nonzero extrinsic curvature as a curve in ℝ⁴. This extrinsic curvature modifies the area swept by the dual face as the primal edge is deformed — a correction to the Hodge weight beyond the circumcenter calculation. For arcs of length \(2\pi/3\) and \(\pi/2\) on unit S³, this is a computable geometric correction in closed form.

5.2 Five-Valent Vertex Junctions

At each of K6's 6 vertices, exactly 5 edges meet. Kirchhoff's current law at a 5-edge junction leaves one degree of freedom that cannot be balanced locally: the currents at 4 edges can be paired, but the fifth is unconstrained without a global condition. This residual drives a non-local magnetic flux correction to the Maxwell action. Unlike the Hodge weight corrections (which are local, edge-by-edge), this is a vertex correction — it depends on the full connectivity of K6 around each vertex. It is computable from the K6 adjacency structure and requires no new geometric input beyond what is already established. The contribution is expected to be of order \(1/(2\pi)\) per vertex, giving \(6/(2\pi) \approx 0.95\) — of the right order of magnitude to close a significant part of the remaining gap.

5.3 Temporal Hodge Weights

The calculations so far are purely spatial (3D Hodge star on S³). Electromagnetism in 4D requires the temporal Hodge weight as well — the mapping between the electric field (a 1-form in 4D spacetime) and its dual (a 3-form). This requires embedding the time-like edges of K6/QS3 in 4D Minkowski space and computing their dual volumes. The SL(2,ℂ) boost structure (Section 5 of Papers 1-2) gives the time-like edge directions; their dual faces are the natural next computation.

6. The Seifert Surface as a Consistency Check

From prior work (v8.2 working notes), the H2 Seifert surface correction gives \(\Delta(1/\alpha) = -120\,\Gamma_{H2} = -1.364\) — matching the required correction to 99.97%. The formula uses the dilogarithm \(\text{Li}_2(e^{-2\alpha})\) and the factor \(120 = |S_5|\). While numerically excellent, this derivation uses \(e^{-2\alpha}\) in the wave function weighting and is therefore not fully alpha-free. It provides strong geometric evidence that the Seifert surface is the correct mechanism. Rederiving the factor 120 from K6 topology alone — without using the known \(\alpha\) as input — is a primary target for this paper's next version.
